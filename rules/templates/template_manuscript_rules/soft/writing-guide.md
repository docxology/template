# Writing Guide

These are **soft** guidelines for writing research manuscripts. Apply them
with judgment; surface deviations as suggestions, not hard blockers.

---

## Voice and Tone

- Write in a formal, precise, and impersonal academic register.
- Use **active voice** where possible: "We show that…" rather than "It is
  shown that…".
- Avoid colloquialisms, contractions, and informal language.
- Aim for concision: every sentence should earn its place.

---

## Clarity

- Define technical terms on first use.
- Avoid unexplained acronyms: spell out on first occurrence, then abbreviate.
- Prefer shorter sentences (target ≤ 30 words) for complex technical content.
- Use parallel structure in lists, headings, and comparisons.

---

## Objectivity

- Present evidence before conclusions.
- Acknowledge limitations explicitly in a dedicated subsection.
- Distinguish between your contributions and related work clearly.
- Avoid superlatives ("best", "perfect", "revolutionary") without quantitative
  support.

---

## Figures and Tables

- Every figure and table must be referenced in the main text before it appears.
- Captions must be self-contained: a reader should understand the figure
  without reading the surrounding text.
- Use vector graphics (PDF, SVG) for plots; raster (PNG ≥ 300 dpi) for
  photographs.

---

## Mathematical Notation

- Define all symbols before use.
- Use consistent notation throughout; do not reuse a symbol with different
  meanings.
- Number all displayed equations.

---

## Revision Checklist (Soft)

Before submitting, verify (by judgment, not automated gate):

- [ ] Abstract accurately reflects the full paper.
- [ ] Introduction states the problem, contributions, and structure.
- [ ] Related work is up to date (within 2 years of submission).
- [ ] Conclusion does not introduce new claims.
- [ ] All figures/tables are cited.
- [ ] Acknowledgements and author contributions are present.
