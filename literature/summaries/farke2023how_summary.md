**Overview/Summary**

The paper by [Authors] presents a study on how observing online activities and applying advertising inferences to those activities impacts people's concerns about the amount of information Google collects from them. The authors conducted an experiment where participants were asked to label their online activities, such as browsing YouTube videos or searching for recipes, and then observed the true interest that was assigned by Google (a "summarized" version of their activities) and compared it with how they thought Google would apply labels. They found that people's concerns about the amount of information collected from them did not change after observing their online activities and applying ad interests to those activities, but their concerns actually decreased when they observed their true interest as assigned by Google.

**Key Contributions/Findings**

The study is based on a survey of 170 participants who were asked to label their online activities. The authors found that the majority of the participants did not change their initial assessment after doing the tasks. However, among those who changed their assessment, 29 people moved up in concern and 27 people moved down. The main finding of this study is that when people observed their true interest as assigned by Google, they became less concerned about the amount of information collected from them.

**Methodology/Approach**

The authors conducted an experiment where participants were asked to label their online activities. In the first part of the survey, participants were asked to rate how much they are concerned with the amount of information that Google collects from them on a 5-point Likert scale (from Not at all Concerned to Extremely Concerned). The second part of the survey was the labeling task where participants were asked to label their online activities. After completing the labeling task, participants were asked again how much they are concerned with the amount of information that Google collects from them on a 5-point Likert scale (from Not at all Concerned to Extremely Concerned). The authors also collected demographic data such as age and gender.
