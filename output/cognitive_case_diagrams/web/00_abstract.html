Linguistic case is the formal inflectional and syntactic encoding of participant roles—such as agent, patient, and instrument—that structures semantic dependencies across the world’s languages. This manuscript presents a wide-scoping review of theoretical and applied linguistics, synthesizing categorial grammar, compositional semantics, enriched category theory, and topos-theoretic logic within a modern computational and cognitive methodology. From the foundational Kāraka theory of Pāṇini and Charles Fillmore’s universal deep cases through to the contemporary alignment typologies of Polinsky and Preminger, the grammatical space of case systems exhibits a rigorous algebraic topology. Category theory provides the native mathematical architecture for this structure: thematic roles manifest as objects, grammatical derivations as morphisms, and cross-linguistic alignment variations as structure-preserving functors. The residuated syntactic calculus of Lambek, simplified into pregroup grammars, instantiates sentence derivations as compact closed categories, rendering complex grammatical operations visually transparent through Joyal and Street’s string diagrams. The DisCoCat framework functorially maps these abstract syntactic derivations into concrete vector spaces, generating empirically testable compositional semantics, while its topological successor, DisCoCirc, expands this tensor network logic to multi-sentence discourse coherence via persistent entity wires. The integration of categorical magnitude over \([0,1]\)-enriched hom-sets translates semantic proximity into information-theoretic invariants of linguistic complexity. Furthermore, Caramello’s topos-theoretic bridges guarantee structural translation across divergent formalisms, ensuring that invariants established in one grammatical theory port seamlessly to another. Embedding these linguistic traditions within the biophysics of active inference reveals case-marked commutative diagrams not merely as descriptive notation, but as the generative models cognitive systems deploy to minimize surprisal during embodied action and linguistic comprehension. The synthetic CEREBRUM architecture instantiates this unification computationally by assigning case roles as functional constraints within an active inference cycle. The cognitive science of diagrammatic reasoning—from Larkin and Simon’s computational analyses to Shimojima’s free-ride inferences—establishes these tensor networks as cognitively privileged representations offering deep inferential advantages. Ultimately, categorical compositional structure supplies the mathematical bedrock for scalable, interpretable artificial intelligence, demonstrating that the identical relational algebras governing human grammatical case assignment inherently support structured multi-agent coordination, linguistic generation, and dynamic environmental adaptation.
